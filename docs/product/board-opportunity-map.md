# The AI-Native HOA Board: Opportunity Map

**A living document.** Built from a full read of the Ridge Top Terrace (RTT) HOA board's email
corpus, December 2025 (when Mike joined the board) through July 12, 2026 — roughly 165
substantive threads. Every claim below cites a real thread (subject + date) so it can be
audited. Add to the Learnings Log (§8) as we discover more; don't rewrite history above it.

**North star:** the AI-native HOA board — automation and applied intelligence that
dramatically reduce the workload and mental weight carried by volunteer board members.

**Privacy note:** this document contains real addresses and names from board business.
It is internal. Redact before sharing outside the board/product team.

---

## 1. How this board actually runs today

RTT is a 120-unit townhouse HOA in Fairfax, VA — 43 units (36%) are absentee/investor-owned
rentals ("RTT HOA absentee and investor owner properties," 6/21). Three volunteer board
members run everything through personal email inboxes:

| Actor | Role | What the email shows |
|---|---|---|
| Johan (president) | Carries the load | Initiates ~70% of all threads. Tracks open items by hand ("I am going through various pending items and couldn't find…", 4/21). Chases everyone. |
| Jugnu (treasurer) | Financial review, minutes, agenda | Overloaded; emails sit in drafts ("I thought I had sent this email out earlier, however, it was still sitting in my drafts," 3/31); needed help finding where financials even come from (5/15–5/18). |
| Mike (member) | Tech; built the inspection app | Joined Dec 2025. Missed ARC reviews for weeks ("Sorry I missed this," 5/14). |

Around them, a constellation the board must supervise but doesn't control:

- **Capitol Management** (management company): Krystal (property manager — letters, notices,
  mailings, records), Kathy (finance — invoices, transfers, journal entries), Angela
  (statements, legal status reports), Peyton.
- **Chadwick Washington** (legal): Olga (counsel — training, memos, policy review), Valerie
  (collections).
- **Vendors:** Shenandoah (landscape/snow/trees), JDR Towing, Palmer Brothers (signs),
  Aurora (paving), Patriot (trash).
- **Financial rails:** Morgan Stanley (Hamilton — reserves/CDs), Goldklang CPAs (taxes/audit),
  Enumerate (the accounting system that emails monthly report packages).
- **The "system of record":** Gmail threads + a shared Google Drive folder + a shared Google
  calendar + Word docs versioned by filename ("just add it in and rename the document +MdH,"
  5/14). Decisions live in reply chains. Institutional memory lives in Johan's head.

**The defining fact:** everything — violations, hearings, votes, invoices, vendor work,
policy, complaints — is a forwarded email with "FYSA" on top. The board's real job is
*state tracking across dozens of open loops*, and email is the worst possible database
for it.

## 2. The recurring loops and what they cost

### 2.1 Violation → letter → hearing (the heaviest loop)

The full lifecycle, reconstructed from four real cases:

1. Resident reports or board observes → forwarded to Krystal with instruction to send a
   violation letter ("RTT HOA Visitor tag 0123," 2/18).
2. Silence. Board manually polls: "What is the status of this hearing?" (4/8); "I've sent a
   few messages about this property" (7/6); "Following up again… They were due for a hearing
   and we haven't seen a letter or date scheduled" (6/29).
3. Hearing scheduling = date ping-pong: Krystal offers dates → each member replies
   availability → Johan picks → Krystal sends notice, hand-checking the statutory advance
   notice ("With 15 days notice we can do a hearing anytime after July…", 7/7). The 11403
   Abner chain took 5 messages just to land on July 28, and Mike never actually replied.
4. Surprises land at the worst moment: the 11436 Abner (marijuana) hearing was **cancelled
   the day of** when the tenant's statement arrived hours before (4/8), triggering a legal
   review for formal dismissal.

Cost: 3–5 threads and 3+ weeks per case; 11403 Abner's underlying issue "dates back to
April" (7/7) — three months from report to hearing notice. Status exists only in Krystal's
files ("I could not find that license plate in my files," 5/29).

### 2.2 Board decisions with no system of record

Votes happen as email concurrence chains: "Approved." (2/3), "I concur" (5/7), "Jugnu do you
concur?" (7/7), and once **by text message**, papered over with a confirming email
("Per our communication by text today… confirming our unanimous decision," 6/26). Then they
must be ratified at the next meeting — which means someone must remember them.

The failure mode, verbatim (tree remediation proposal, 5/7): Jugnu: "I concur. I thought we
did provide con-currence." Johan: "I went through my pending items and don't see concurrence
on this proposal." A decision evaporated and had to be re-voted. Minutes are a Word file
emailed around so Jugnu can fill in "who seconded the motions" (4/30 → finalized 5/13).

### 2.3 Financial oversight by human diff

The board receives a monthly packet (Enumerate financial + bank statement + legal status
report from Angela) and reviews it line by line. Errors they caught **only because a human
stared at PDFs**:

- Unrecorded $1,099 reserve auto-transfer, two months running — "Sorry that was my fault…
  I forgot" (Kathy, 2/9); explanation still being chased 3/30.
- Transfer rounding error requiring a Morgan Stanley correction entry and an audit-trail
  lecture — "It is no wonder errors occur" (3/27–3/31).
- Mystery $102 returned-check fee (3/10); unexplained bank entries again in July ("Returned
  check fee, Deluxe…", 7/7).
- Quarterly taxes paid on the due date / a day late (1/6); a surprise "historical
  bookkeeping correction" appearing on financials the board never approved (4/9).
- **Substitute reports**: "We pay for automated accounting and bookkeeping services. Too
  often there are substitute pdf produced to account for errors" (7/7) — i.e., the system
  of record is being patched around, and only vigilance notices.

The board also runs an exact-amount funds-request procedure entirely on email discipline
("Please send us a separate email listing the payee, amount, and a total," 3/25), which
Capitol keeps violating.

### 2.4 Compliance nobody is watching

- The HOA's **license expired January 31 and nobody noticed until March 30**, when Johan
  happened to check online; the DPOR filing also listed the wrong officers ("this has
  happened before with excuses," 3/30).
- The state corporation submission was "wrong again" in May; the board voted that the
  management company may no longer sign state/federal filings without board review (5/7–5/21).
- A lien memorandum went out for the wrong signer because the December officer update never
  propagated (3/9).
- Johan's stated wish: "I would like to develop a **due dates matrix** so we can track when
  things are due, how to access items on line, and filing deadlines" (5/15).
- The annual insurance/demographics/leases data call has been "late and dragged out" three
  years running, so Johan now pre-writes the timeline for Krystal (6/19–6/22).

### 2.5 Institutional memory is a person

- The board-approved cost schedule: asked 5/18, answered **July 7**: "I did a little digging…
  could only find the attached draft on file… this seems to be under previous management."
  Never updated since the management company changed. Now a July agenda item.
- Association data "has been fragmented, incomplete" since 2023 (6/17); the owner roster is
  stale ("Krystal hasn't kept it current," 6/22).
- Policy references live in memory: "the resolution itself from 2015" (2/25), a process doc
  re-forwarded from an August 2025 email (5/18).

### 2.6 ARC applications fall through the cracks

Three applications in the window; two sat unanswered for weeks while owners waited:
deck replacement submitted 4/2 — owner "tentatively scheduled the work for this Friday"
(4/8) — still open 5/14 ("Is this one still open as well?"); 4152 Zinnia tree removal
submitted 4/22, owner nudged 5/5, answered 5/14 ("Sorry I missed this"). There is no queue,
no SLA, no reminder — just an email to two volunteers.

### 2.7 Vendors, spend, and the rest

- Invoice approval: Kathy forwards, "Please approve," Johan replies "Approved." — plus probing
  questions because "there were multiple rate errors last year" (2/3).
- Work orders flow through Krystal by prose (sign repair 2/20; "use the same metal pole").
  Quotes need wet signatures chased across members (2/26–3/3).
- Vendor performance disputes escalate to counsel: Aurora's striping "started flaking"
  → contract review → legal analysis memo → negotiation plan (6/14–7/1). Grounds work
  "sloppy or unsupervised… We pay them a significant amount each year" (6/23).
- Snow season = ~18 broadcast threads of vendor status noise burying real work (Jan–Mar).
- Parking/towing: policy = 72h or 3x/month, but "it is difficult for the board to monitor
  and residents have submitted their own lists" (2/19). Johan halted all towing pending
  legal review (2/26), then redesigned the process by email including a board-suggested
  photo-of-the-tag requirement (5/18–5/30).
- Community letters: agreed valuable (counsel: "Most people don't read the rules closely,"
  2/25) — yet a researched parking letter was later found **never sent** ("it doesn't look
  like this was sent out. It needs to be resent," 6/25), and a meeting notice went to
  Capitol staff instead of owners (5/27).

### 2.8 Proof the model works: the inspection app is already live

In June the board ran its annual inspection cycle **inside the product** (Johan, Laura, and
committee member Mike Yim as inspectors). Johan, unprompted: "Mike what you've done will cut
down on all the back and forth on issuing the letters" (4/28) and "App is very helpful"
(6/15). Their friction reports are a free usability study:

| Report | Thread | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Voice-to-text broken for all 3 inspectors | 6/14–6/15 | Fixed by ui-redesign (native dictation) |
| Street shows grey 0/9 after marking homes complete | 6/15, 6/18 | Status-model confusion; ui-redesign's "Next house" sheet addresses the flow |
| Letter template editing + preview with real bullets | 6/22–6/26 | Preview exists; surface it better |
| Exported letters missing name/address block | 7/6 | Bug — fix before next cycle |
| Photo storage filled up silently | 6/25 | Fixed; needs monitoring/alerting |
| "Good idea to do a QC check before the PDFs are generated" | 6/18 | = the Walkthrough review flow |
| Offline backup/export after cycle completes | 6/26 | Roadmap: cycle-end archive export |

This is the wedge: the board already trusts the product with its highest-stakes annual
process. Every opportunity below extends from that beachhead.

## 4. The three costliest loops (where AI changes the game)

1. **The follow-up tax.** Johan's core activity is remembering open loops and polling humans
   for state ("Going through pending items…" appears in some form in at least six threads).
   Product answer: everything is a case with a stage, an owner, and a due date; the system
   does the chasing. This is opportunity #1+#2 and it's already built — shipping it converts
   the board's biggest cost immediately.
2. **The decision that never happened.** Email concurrence is quorum-fragile, unsearchable,
   and evaporates (§2.2). Product answer: #4 decision log — small to build, huge mental-weight
   relief, and legally meaningful (ratification lists, minutes evidence).
3. **The silent compliance bomb.** An expired license is exactly the class of failure that
   costs real money and nobody's job is to watch. Product answer: #5 compliance calendar with
   AI verification — the most differentiated "AI-native" surface because it does work no
   volunteer was ever going to do reliably.

## 5. Transition playbook: email-only board → product

The RTT story is the repeatable go-to-market: don't ask a board to adopt software — absorb
their existing email behavior one loop at a time, each stage paying for the next.

**Stage 0 — A wedge tool for the worst annual pain.** *(Done at RTT.)* Inspections + letters
replaced the clipboard-and-Krystal cycle. Lesson: boards adopt when a respected member
champions it and the first workflow is one they already dread. Keep the champion's
feedback loop tight (Johan filed 7 usability reports in 3 weeks — gold).

**Stage 1 — Track the open loops.** Promote case tracking + hearings to prod. Seed the live
sagas (11403 Abner, 11493 Abner, towing repeat-violator) as cases with real history. Invite
Krystal as a member — the management company is in every loop, so give Capitol a seat
instead of routing around them; their work (letters, notices, mailings) becomes case events.
Success: zero "following up again" emails; time-to-hearing measured, not guessed.

**Stage 2 — Make email the front door, not the database.** Enable email intake: the board
forwards exactly as they do today, AI files it. ARC applications from Krystal land in the
ARC queue with SLAs. Add the decision log; "Reply-concur" chains get captured as motions.
Success: a month where no decision or application exists only in an inbox.

**Stage 3 — Absorb the calendar.** Meetings (agenda accretion → agenda → minutes draft) and
the compliance calendar. Success: July-meeting agenda assembles itself from the cycle's
cases and pending ratifications; every filing deadline has an owner and a verified state.

**Stage 4 — The intelligence layer.** Financial packet reviewer, vendor/work-order history,
institutional-memory RAG. These need Stages 1–3's structured data to be trustworthy.
Success: the monthly financial review starts from AI-flagged questions; the next
vendor RFP is built from recorded performance history.

**Adoption principles observed at RTT:**
- The champion sells it internally ("I love this progress and innovation" — Johan, 4/28).
- Never lose their data trust: Johan asked about backups within weeks (6/26). Cycle-end
  exports and visible durability are adoption features, not ops chores.
- Meet their vocabulary: FYSA, concurrence, ratification, data call. The UI should speak
  board, not SaaS.
- Volunteer time is bursty (drafts sit for weeks; vacations break quorum) — async-first,
  reminder-driven design, never meeting-dependent.

## 7. Open questions

- Multi-tenant sensitivity: Krystal manages many associations under Capitol — is she a user
  of RTT's instance, or the beachhead for a management-company portfolio product later?
  (Portfolio view is parked; this corpus suggests PMs are the highest-leverage user.)
- Legal-privilege handling: several threads are attorney-client privileged. Intake must
  classify and wall these off (visibility controls exist on case notes — is that enough?).
- How far can the financial reviewer go without becoming an accounting product? (Position:
  reviewer/asker, never ledger.)
- E-bike/policy work shows the board also produces *governance artifacts* (resolutions,
  memos). Does the decision log grow a lightweight policy register?
- Does hearing scheduling integrate calendars (the board already shares a Google calendar)
  or stay availability-polling with smarter defaults?

## 8. Learnings log (append-only)

- **2026-07-12** — Initial map built from Dec 2025–Jul 2026 corpus (~165 threads; method:
  month-by-month Gmail thread inventory + snippet-level read; raw index in session
  scratchpad `email-thread-index.md`). Key surprises: (1) the app is further along in real
  adoption than the roadmap assumed — three inspectors completed the June cycle in it;
  (2) compliance failure (expired license) was the single scariest finding and nothing in
  the product roadmap addressed it before this doc; (3) the management company is not the
  adversary — they're the most active participant and the likeliest power user.
